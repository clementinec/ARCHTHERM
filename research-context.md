# Research Context To Reuse

This file records how Hongshan Guo's public research profile should inform the course without turning the course into a private research seminar.

Primary reference: FORGE, Future, Occupant, Risk, and Generative Environments, <https://thermalcomfort.zone/>.

## Public Research Signals

FORGE describes itself as a design-science lab for human-building-climate systems at The University of Hong Kong. Its public focus includes standards revision, future weather uncertainty, thermal comfort prediction, AI-supported design pedagogy, EnergyPlus, climate forcing, Monte Carlo, mean radiant temperature, stochastic weather, sensitivity analysis, and co-simulation.

The research page frames the lab around connected lines of work:

- human heat, comfort, and standards
- climate futures and stochastic weather
- operation, ventilation, and design-facing systems
- thermal comfort, human heat, and MRT
- control, co-simulation, and interpretable learning
- ventilation, public institutions, and design-facing AI

The course should translate these into student-facing problems:

| Research signal | Course translation |
|---|---|
| human heat and standards | default occupant critique; vulnerability and fairness |
| mean radiant temperature | radiant plans, sections, surface temperature, shade, window-seat evidence |
| future weather uncertainty | weather-file stress tests and failure-hour comparisons |
| EnergyPlus/co-simulation | inspectable hourly exposure states |
| ordinal/probabilistic comfort | uncertainty-aware comfort/protection claims |
| missing-data and input-quality sensitivity | evidence quality and model failure |
| ventilation and public buildings | institutional thermal conditions, mixed-mode spaces, wet-market/public-space analogues |
| design-facing AI/pedagogy | reproducibility, Socratic critique, and defensible workflow arguments |

## Course Boundary

The course should be research-led, not research-substituted. Students are not reproducing the instructor's papers. They are learning a design method:

1. identify a thermal claim
2. sample or simulate the thermal field
3. map spatial/temporal heterogeneity
4. expose uncertainty
5. test failure modes
6. translate the result into architectural action
