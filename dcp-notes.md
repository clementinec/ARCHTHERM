# DCP Notes

This is not a final DCP. It is a holding file for accreditation-safe framing, CLOs, and course description language.

## Draft Course Description

This course examines thermal environments as spatial, temporal, radiant, and human-centered phenomena across indoor, outdoor, and threshold conditions. Rather than treating comfort as a single target value, students learn to interpret air and humidity fields, radiant exchange, thermal capacity, accumulated exposure, measurement uncertainty, model assumptions, weather sequences, and occupant variability as design problems. The course links environmental sensing, field evidence, portable thermodynamic and building-physics equations, simulation outputs, visualization, roll-forward validation, and architectural intervention. Students develop a thermal design action package that translates evidence into one defensible decision about form, envelope, section, shade, material, program, air movement, or control under present and future stress assumptions.

## Draft Learning Outcomes

By the end of the course, students should be able to:

1. Explain thermal environments as interactions among air, surfaces, radiation, humidity, air movement, bodies, behavior, and time.
2. Distinguish air temperature from radiant temperature, operative temperature, surface temperature, and thermal sensation.
3. Map spatial air-temperature and humidity heterogeneity in indoor, outdoor, or threshold environments.
4. Interpret radiant exchange using surface temperature, mean radiant temperature, body position, and heat-flux logic.
5. Explain thermal build-up, thermal capacity, lag, conduction, and accumulated exposure using portable equations.
6. Collect, simulate, or interpret thermal evidence while identifying measurement, sampling, model, radiant, and assumption uncertainty.
7. Stress-test a building condition under alternative weather, occupant, service, and model assumptions.
8. Conduct a bounded roll-forward validation by testing or reasoning how a design action behaves under changed climate, weather-window, or stress assumptions.
9. Diagnose thermal failure modes hidden by averages, defaults, or single compliance metrics.
10. Construct a bounded design-action ladder and identify sufficient, censored, or unresolved outcomes within a stated action space.
11. Translate thermal evidence into architectural decisions involving form, envelope, shade, material, section, program, landscape, air movement, and control.
12. Defend a thermal design action with drawings, numerical evidence, roll-forward validation, uncertainty statements, and clear limits of claim.

## DCP Cautions

- Do not make the course sound like HVAC training.
- Do not overpromise professional EnergyPlus mastery.
- Do not frame the course as simplified science for designers.
- Do not let sensing become gadget demonstration without architectural translation.
- Do not hide the equations; keep them portable and tied to drawings, plots, and design actions.
- Do not let optional CFD or fire-engineering analogies become required workload.
- Require climate-stress awareness in A4, but do not require decade-by-decade climate redesign.
- Do not hide rigor, but attach every technical concept to an architectural consequence.
- Keep final outputs visible as drawings, maps, plots, time-series, action ladders, and defended design claims.
