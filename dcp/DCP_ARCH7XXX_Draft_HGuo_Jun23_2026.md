## I. Course Description

This elective introduces thermal environments as spatial, temporal, radiant, and human-centered fields that architects can read, test, and transform. Rather than treating comfort as a single indoor setpoint, students learn to interpret air temperature, humidity, surface temperature, mean radiant temperature, air movement, thermal capacity, accumulated exposure, weather sequences, and occupant variability as architectural design questions.

The course is scientifically rigorous and architecturally situated. Each technical idea is tied to a spatial consequence: a perimeter seat, shaded threshold, roof-exposed room, courtyard, glass facade, pavement surface, ventilated section, or thermal refuge. Students learn portable thermodynamic and building-physics concepts through diagrams, field evidence, sensing protocols, simple calculations, simulation outputs, and design-action tests. The aim is not to train students as thermal engineers or HVAC specialists. The aim is to develop thermal intelligence: the ability to see where thermal conditions vary, why they vary, what evidence can or cannot prove, and how design decisions alter exposure, comfort, risk, and environmental legibility.

Across the semester, students build four linked artifacts. They first map spatial air and humidity differences, then examine radiant exchange and body exposure, then audit temporal build-up and failure thresholds, and finally assemble a mechanism-informed thermal design intervention. The final project asks each student to translate thermal evidence into one defensible architectural action involving form, envelope, shade, material, section, program, landscape, air movement, or control. A required roll-forward validation note tests whether the proposed action still makes sense under a changed weather, climate-stress, occupancy, or evaluation assumption.

The course supports accreditation-facing learning in environmental systems, technical knowledge, health, safety and welfare, climate resilience, and research-based design. Its distinctive contribution to architectural education is to move students from single-average environmental reasoning toward spatially explicit, uncertainty-aware, future-facing thermal judgment.

## III. Schedule of Teaching and Learning Activities

The semester uses a repeated structure: Session 1 introduces concepts and worked examples; Session 2 runs a diagnostic round in which students post a design image, classmates make educated guesses under the weekly thermal lens, and the host reveals the actual issue, evidence, mitigation, and uncertainty.

| Week | In-class focus (2h) | Out-of-class work | Key concepts / tools | Assignment link |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| W1 / Air Is Not Enough | Course framing; why one air-temperature value cannot define a thermal condition; Ta/RH, air speed, stagnant air, humidity stress, ventilation context, point-to-field mapping, psychrometric thinking, hidden defaults. Diagnostic round: thermal claim autopsy from a studio or precedent image. | Select one indoor, outdoor, or threshold condition; prepare A1 field question and candidate positions. | Ta, RH, air speed, humidity, psychrometrics, point/contour field mapping, stagnant air, hidden defaults, thermal claim autopsy. | A1 launch |
| W2 / Body Heat Balance | How a body exchanges heat with a space; metabolism, clothing, convection, draft/breeze, radiation, evaporation, dehumidification, PMV/PPD, adaptive comfort, UTCI, WBGT, and heat-index concepts as models with domains and limits. Diagnostic round: identify dominant heat-exchange pathway and competing design constraints. | Draft body-space heat-balance sketch; refine sensing or observation protocol. | Human heat balance, air speed, local body exposure, PMV/PPD, adaptive comfort, heat stress indices, physiology, design consequence. | A1 evidence setup |
| W3 / Sensing Thermal Fields | What sensing can prove; sensor placement, logging interval, sampling bias, interpolation, point maps, contour/heat maps, air-movement observation, surface readings, radiant proxy options, ventilation versus local exposure, privacy and field ethics. ### A4 Scope

A4 is assembled from A1-A3. It should not be restarted from scratch in Weeks 10-12. The final package asks each student to choose one thermal condition and one bounded action route:

- room or interior condition;
- facade, envelope, or roof edge;
- outdoor or threshold microclimate;
- operational, air-movement, or control choice.

Every A4 submission must include one roll-forward validation note. The required version changes one stress assumption, such as hotter week, heatwave period, changed occupancy, changed window/glazing assumption, current versus future-adjusted weather, or different stress-window length. Students label the outcome as robust, weakened, censored, or unsupported. Multi-window or multi-weather reruns are encouraged for distinction-level work when they improve architectural judgment, but they are not required for basic completion.

## IX. Statement of Academic Conduct

All written work in this course will be submitted for plagiarism review via Turnitin, at http://turnitin.com. Clarification of the University of Hong Kong's policies on plagiarism, as well as detailed descriptions of how to properly cite and source material in written work and examinations, is available at http://www.hku.hk/plagiarism. Plagiarism includes handing in the work of another as one's own, and failure to appropriately cite sources. Plagiarism is an academic misdemeanor, and may be considered grounds for failure from this course as well as further disciplinary action from the University.

Students must adhere to HKU policies on academic integrity, research ethics, privacy, and responsible use of digital tools. All submissions must be original and properly cited. Where AI assistance is used to generate or assess text, code, figures, scripts, calculations, or workflow components, students must declare the use, state what was checked, and retain enough process documentation for review. AI outputs must not be treated as final evidence. Factual claims should be checked against reliable sources; relationship-based claims should be checked against physical reasoning, empirical evidence, sensitivity tests, or bounded calculations; workflow/code outputs should be run and tested with known cases.

Course-specific expectations:

1. Testability - if direct data are unavailable, students should test the physics, spatial logic, or threshold rule behind the claim with a small proxy, prepared output, transparent comparison, or bounded calculation.
2. Evidence tiers - students should state whether evidence is field observation, sensor data, precedent, standard, proxy test, simulation, or professional assumption.
3. Clarity over complexity - a correct map, table, or time-series is better than an opaque technical workflow.
4. Decision threshold - students should state what result would justify adopting, revising, or deferring a design move.
5. Data ethics and privacy - if people, images, phones, sensors, or identifiable behavior are involved, students should minimize collection, avoid intrusive monitoring, remove names/IDs, store files appropriately, and state a deletion date where relevant.
6. Reproducibility - figures, thresholds, and claims should be traceable from the submitted capsule.
7. Limits of claim - students should clearly state what was not measured, not simulated, averaged away, or outside the tested action space.
