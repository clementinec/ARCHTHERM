Thermal Fields in Architecture

Bodies · Moisture · Radiation · Climate Stress

Thermal design is not the pursuit of one average indoor condition. It is the shaping of heterogeneous thermal environments across bodies, surfaces, rooms, buildings, and climates under uncertainty.

Students learn to map thermal variation, stress-test buildings against assumptions and climate scenarios, identify failure modes hidden by averages or compliance metrics, and translate evidence into architectural interventions.

A 12-week MArch elective at HKU (Hongshan Guo). Research-led, but the assessed work stays architectural: you build a small thermal evidence deck and use it to defend one mechanism-informed design intervention.

Schedule

Twelve weeks, two-session classes, what is due when.

Weekly Decks

Lecture + a Session-2 thermal diagnostic round.

Assignments

A1 → A4, one accumulating evidence deck.

Resources

Equations, sensing kit, weather data, and tools.

The semester asks four questions

Every week attaches one thermal mechanism to one architectural object — a window seat, courtyard, roof slab, shaded bench, facade edge, or a vulnerable occupant.

01

What is the thermal condition?

Not “the room is 24 °C.” A thermal condition is a sampled field — which location, hour, surface, body, weather, and model.

02

Where does it vary?

Spatial heterogeneity is the architectural core. Plan, section, surface, and body all matter.

03

When does it vary?

Thermal design is choreography, not a compliance snapshot — diurnal swings, heatwaves, failure hours.

04

What does design do?

Shade, expose, shelter, ventilate, relocate, re-section. Compliance is not protection.

One accumulating evidence deck

You are not asked to restart at a final project. Each artifact builds on the last into a single defended argument.

Weeks Artifact Proves
1–3 Condition Card + Spatial Air Field Map the condition is specific, and the air/humidity field varies by position
4–5 Radiant Exchange Card the same air temperature can mean different body heat exchange
6–9 Thermal Build-Up Audit + System Boundary Box heat accumulates, lags, or exceeds thresholds inside a declared spatial, temporal, service, and climate boundary
9–10 Design Action Ladder a bounded action space — found, censored, or unresolved
10–12 Roll-Forward Validation + Design Action Memo the action holds (or weakens) under a changed climate stress
12 Reproducibility Capsule another person can inspect or re-run the evidence

Portable physics — a few equations, used architecturally

You will not become a thermal engineer. You will learn a small set of equations well enough to reason with them.

Physics idea Concept Architectural consequence
spatial difference ΔT = T_B − T_A one room or threshold is not one condition
conduction q = U·A·ΔT envelope area, U-value, and temperature difference matter
stored heat Q = m·c·ΔT material capacity creates thermal build-up and lag
heat balance C·dT/dt = gains − losses shade, ventilation, insulation, and gains change time behaviour
accumulated exposure degree-hours = Σ max(T − T_threshold, 0) duration and tail hours matter, not annual averages
radiant exchange q_rad ≈ h_r·A·(T_surface − T_body) surface temperature and body position change perceived heat

From a thermal finding to an architectural action

The payoff is translation — evidence becoming one defensible design move.

Thermal finding Architectural translation
Perimeter zone overheats in the afternoon external shading, lower SHGC, program relocation, facade depth
Air temperature passes but MRT fails surface-temperature control, glazing strategy, radiant-asymmetry mitigation
Roof-exposed room fails during a heatwave roof insulation, cool roof, ventilated cavity, night purge
Average comfort passes but tail hours fail design for extreme periods, not the annual average
One weather file passes, a future file fails climate stress-test; robust rather than optimised design
A local breeze or shaded humid threshold is beneficial organize the difference as refuge, choice, or transition rather than flattening it

How class runs

Each two-hour class is a lecture/tool demo, then a studio-lab.

Session 2 is a thermal diagnostic round, not a generic discussion. A student hosts one design image; the class makes educated guesses; the host reveals the actual thermal question, evidence, mitigation, and limits. The question is never “is this a bad spot?” — it is what mechanism is this design organising, ignoring, or worsening, and what intervention acts on it?

What you will be able to say

I can see thermal environments as uneven, uncertain, embodied, and designed — and translate one thermal finding into one architectural action without overclaiming.
