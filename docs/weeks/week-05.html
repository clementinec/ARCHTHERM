A2 asks students to translate radiant difference into an architectural claim: not “it feels different,” but “this surface, exposure, or orientation changes the body’s heat exchange.”

Direction Matters

A body is not a sphere floating in an abstract room.

Radiant asymmetry appears when one side of the body faces a different radiant environment than another side.

Radiant exchange is shaped by view exposure:

\[ q_{rad,i} \propto F_i(T_i^4 - T_{body}^4) \]

F_i is the fraction of the body’s radiant view occupied by surface or source i.

Facade And Ground Property Levers

Architects do work with these properties, even when the language is hidden inside facade, roof, paving, and coating decisions.

Lever What changes Useful question
higher solar reflectance less absorbed shortwave heat where does reflected radiation go next?
higher absorptivity more solar energy becomes surface heat does it warm a winter refuge or worsen a summer burden?
higher emissivity stronger longwave exchange does it support night cooling or radiate heat toward occupants?
lower thermal capacity faster response, less stored release does it cool quickly after sunset?
higher thermal capacity delayed peak, stored heat does it stabilize or prolong discomfort?
surface texture / geometry view factor, self-shading, convection does roughness help cooling or trap heat/dirt/moisture?

Reflective surfaces can lower their own temperature while increasing glare or reflected radiant load elsewhere. A good A2 answer follows the energy, not the product label.

Daytime Indoors Is Still Radiant

Indoor radiant variation can be significant during daytime even when the HVAC system holds air temperature near a setpoint.

Indoor condition Radiant mechanism Design question
sun patch through glazing shortwave reaches body, floor, or furniture does shade stop heat at the exterior or only soften glare?
hot perimeter glass warm surface and solar-adjacent radiant field should the body be offset, screened, or locally controlled?
exposed slab / floor stored heat changes surface temperature does mass delay comfort or prolong burden?
cooled interior surface cold radiant sink or asymmetry does it improve comfort or create local discomfort?
radiant panel / chilled ceiling useful surface-temperature strategy how is condensation, control, and body exposure handled?

Radiant design is not only avoiding bad surfaces. It is using surfaces of different temperatures strategically.

Indoor Versus Outdoor Radiant Stakes

Indoor

Radiant asymmetry may be subtle but persistent: cold glass, warm perimeter, radiant panels, exposed slab.

Outdoor

Solar radiation and hot ground can dominate within seconds or minutes.

Threshold

Arcade, lobby, balcony, and entry conditions often mix air-conditioned expectations with outdoor radiant exposure.

Night Sky As Heat Sink

Radiant exchange can cool as well as heat.

At night, surfaces with sky view can lose longwave radiation to the sky. That cooling can support roofs, courtyards, and outdoor surfaces, but it depends on:

  • sky view factor;
  • cloud cover and humidity;
  • surface temperature and emissivity;
  • wind and convective coupling;
  • whether stored daytime heat is still being released.

Urban heat island is partly a nighttime problem: dense surfaces store heat, sky view is reduced, wind paths can be weak, and the city may not release heat fast enough after sunset.

Emissivity And Sky View

Night-sky cooling is strongest when a surface can “see” the sky and exchange longwave radiation effectively.

A design-level reading:

night cooling potential = sky view + emissivity - stored heat - humid/cloudy sky penalty

This is where radiant design links to UHI: material heat storage, sky view, emissivity, wind, and humidity shape whether the city recovers at night.

Surface Strategy, Not Homogeneous Space

Do not assume the goal is one equal temperature everywhere.

Strategy Surface role Architectural reading
shade blocks direct shortwave before it lands prevents radiant load rather than repairing it later
cool surface acts as a radiant sink useful if condensation, glare, and contact comfort are controlled
warm winter surface creates local refuge heterogeneity can be desirable in cool conditions
low-storage paving reduces evening heat release affects night recovery and outdoor use
vegetated / wet edge changes surface temperature and latent context may cool locally while increasing moisture management needs

A2 should teach students to ask: which surfaces should be hot, cool, shaded, exposed, reflective, absorptive, massive, or lightweight, and for whom?

A2 Card Structure

Your Radiant Exchange Card must include:

  1. two occupied positions;
  2. air temperature comparison;
  3. surface/radiant/MRT proxy evidence;
  4. body orientation diagram;
  5. one approximate radiant source/sink note, using wattage, surface temperature, material property, or MRT difference where possible;
  6. one heat-flux or exchange sketch;
  7. one architectural implication;
  8. one uncertainty statement.

What A2 Should Not Do

A2 should not become a generic comfort essay or a polished rendering with no thermal mechanism.

If the drawing cannot answer “what exchange changed?”, it is not yet an A2 argument.

Mini-Case: Shaded Bench

Two benches show similar air temperature:

  • Bench A is under tree shade on lighter paving.
  • Bench B is under partial glass reflection near dark paving.

A2 asks: what does the body receive from above, below, and around?

The simplified comparison:

\[ \Delta q_{rad} \approx h_rA(T_{mrt,B}-T_{mrt,A}) \]

If the MRT proxy differs substantially, the design implication may be shade quality, surface material, or seating relocation.

Session 2: Diagnostic Round

10 min - case scan. Choose a design image where body geometry matters: one side of the body faces a different radiant environment.

5 min - Slack post. Post the image and identify the body posture or orientation. Keep the thermal diagnosis hidden.

20-25 min - round-table guesses. Classmates guess the exposed body side, radiant source or sink, design mitigation, and competing constraint.

10-15 min - host reveal. The host explains the actual radiant asymmetry concern and whether the current design response is sufficient.
