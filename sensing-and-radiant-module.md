# Sensing And Radiant Module

This module should become one of the distinctive course artifacts.

## Purpose

Students learn that air temperature alone is not a thermal condition. They compare air, surface, radiant, and position-dependent evidence so that indoor and outdoor thermal heterogeneity becomes visible in plan, section, time, and body exposure.

## Candidate Hardware Routes

Required fallback:

- air temperature and humidity logger
- infrared thermometer or surface-temperature reading
- manual plan/section position log
- time stamp and photo record

Preferred route:

- globe thermometer or equivalent MRT proxy
- air speed where possible
- surface temperature of key radiant surfaces
- indoor/outdoor or threshold transect

Advanced route:

- SMART-style radiant sensing module associated with Forrest Meggers' research ecosystem, exact device and current specifications to verify
- thermal camera or spherical/hemispherical thermal imagery
- six-direction shortwave/longwave or radiant proxy readings if available

## Minimum Student Exercise

Pick two or three positions that look architecturally similar but should be thermally different.

Examples:

- window seat versus interior desk
- shaded bench versus sunlit bench
- atrium edge versus core corridor
- roof terrace sun patch versus shaded threshold
- glass facade perimeter versus deeper plan
- outdoor pavement versus planted edge

For each position, record:

- location on plan/section
- time
- air temperature
- humidity, if available
- surface temperatures of dominant surrounding surfaces
- globe/SMART/radiant proxy, if available
- air movement observation or measured air speed
- sun/shade condition
- body posture/activity assumption
- short subjective note

## Radiant Translation

Students should translate readings into one of three levels, depending on available data.

### Level 1: Qualitative Radiant Map

Use surface temperatures, sun/shade, material, view exposure, and body position to identify likely radiant hot/cold zones.

### Level 2: MRT Proxy

Use globe temperature, air temperature, air speed, and globe properties to estimate mean radiant temperature. If exact air speed or globe properties are missing, state the uncertainty and compare positions rather than overclaiming absolute MRT.

### Level 3: Radiant Flux / View-Factor Logic

Use surface temperature and approximate view exposure to explain why the body gains or loses radiant heat in one position more than another.

The point is not perfect instrumentation. The point is to show how the result changes architectural judgment.

## Output Format

Students produce a one-page sensing board:

1. plan or section with sensor positions
2. table of readings
3. surface/radiant interpretation
4. one defended claim
5. one uncertainty note
6. one architectural action

## What To Avoid

- treating the sensor as truth
- collecting numbers without a design question
- comparing positions without time stamps
- claiming MRT without stating the method
- ignoring outdoor exposure, surface temperature, or body orientation
- presenting sensing as gadget work rather than architectural evidence
