# Course Thesis

## Core Position

ARCH7XXX teaches architecture students to stop treating thermal performance as a single condition, setpoint, score, or compliance label, and instead read it as a spatial-temporal field whose meaning changes by body, surface, exposure, weather sequence, model assumption, and design intervention.

The course is not:

> a simplified version of thermal science for designers.

It is:

> a rigorous introduction to thermal environmental reasoning for architectural design.

## Clean Thesis

Thermal design is not the pursuit of a single ideal indoor condition. It is the interpretation and shaping of uneven environments across bodies, surfaces, rooms, buildings, and urban spaces under uncertainty.

## Architectural Payoff

Students should leave the course able to say:

> I can see thermal environments as uneven, uncertain, embodied, and designed.

The course should help students stop making naive thermal claims by showing how evidence, metrics, models, and uncertainty change the way architects read a room, facade, courtyard, threshold, street, or body in space.

## Research-To-Teaching Translation

The instructor's research context should enter the course through teaching problems students can see and test:

- default occupants become body, metabolism, vulnerability, and fairness questions
- MRT-aware comfort analysis becomes radiant section, surface temperature, and window-seat evidence
- climate futures become weather-file stress tests and failure-hour comparisons
- EnergyPlus/co-simulation becomes inspectable hourly exposure states, not software credentialing
- ordinal/probabilistic comfort becomes uncertainty-aware design judgment
- sensing becomes spatial evidence: where, when, and how the thermal field was sampled

## Teaching Rule

Start each module with an architectural situation, then unpack the science.

Examples:

- A room is "cold": cold air, cold surfaces, radiant asymmetry, downdraft, low activity, expectation, lack of control, sensor placement, or poor sampling?
- A courtyard is pleasant in the morning and hostile at 2 p.m.: solar exposure, sky view, ground surface temperature, air movement, shade quality, thermal storage, occupant vulnerability, clothing, activity, or duration?
- A simulation says a space is acceptable: acceptable for whom, when, under what assumptions, with what weather file, with what comfort model, and with what spatial averaging?

## Avoid

- designer-friendly
- accessible science
- simplified building physics
- comfort basics
- environmental design for architects

## Prefer

- architecturally actionable
- spatially explicit
- evidence-based
- uncertainty-aware
- human-centered
- thermally heterogeneous
- design-relevant thermal reasoning
- environmental judgment
- performance interpretation
- radiant and microclimatic variation
